A Bit of a Cat-ology

Cats were first domesticated around 7500 BCE in Western Asia and have since become the second most popular domestic pet in the United States. There are over 60 distinct cat breeds, each with its own specific care and dietary needs. Nevertheless, there are general dietary and care recommendations that can promote the health and well-being of your beloved feline, regardless of breed.

More Info

Zero to Four Weeks

During the first four weeks of life, it is crucial for the queen— a term commonly used to describe a female cat that is pregnant or nursing—to directly nurse her kittens whenever possible. Closely monitor each kitten’s growth to ensure they are developing at a steady pace. If any kitten is not gaining weight adequately, a caretaker may need to intervene by feeding it directly with a bottle or a feeding tube. Several factors can contribute to a kitten's inadequate weight gain, including:

  • Competition for milk from numerous siblings

  • Gastrointestinal issues

  • Environmental factors such as extreme temperatures or unsanitary living conditions

Regular observation and timely intervention are key to ensuring the health and wellbeing of the kittens during this critical period.

Four Weeks to One Year

Kittens can usually begin to transition to soft, wet kitten food around three to four weeks after birth. The ASPCA recommends that at this stage, kittens should consume between half a cup to one cup of dry kitten food, or about six to nine ounces of wet kitten food each day. If your kitten struggles with dry food, you can soften it by adding a small amount of water. It's important to choose cat food specifically formulated for kittens, as it contains the essential nutrients needed for their growth, energy, and overall well-being. Keep in mind that dietary requirements may vary based on the breed, so it's always best to consult your veterinarian for personalized recommendations.

One to Seven Years

During this stage, kittens transition into adulthood, requiring fewer nutrients than in their earlier developmental phase. As they mature, you'll notice a decline in their activity levels and metabolism. To promote optimal health, it’s advisable not to leave food out for them throughout the day. Instead, offer meals a few times daily, encouraging them to eat rather than graze. This feeding strategy helps mitigate the risk of obesity and other weight-related health issues in cats.

Seven Years and Beyond

As cats age, their bodies naturally undergo various changes and may begin to deteriorate. At this stage of life, it is essential for them to consume fewer fats and calories, focusing instead on high-quality proteins. When shopping for packaged cat food, prioritize products that specify a particular protein source, such as "salmon," rather than vague categories like "fish." The latter often indicates the presence of byproducts or a mix of lower-quality proteins, which may not provide the best nutrition for your beloved feline.
